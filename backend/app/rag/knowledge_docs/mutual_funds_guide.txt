TITLE: Indian Mutual Funds — Complete Investor Guide

SOURCE: AMFI (Association of Mutual Funds in India), SEBI Circular on MF Categorisation 2017
APPLICABILITY: Retail and HNI mutual fund investors in India

================================================================================
WHAT ARE MUTUAL FUNDS?
================================================================================

A mutual fund pools money from many investors and invests in securities (stocks, bonds, gold,
etc.) managed by a professional fund manager. SEBI regulates all mutual funds in India.
AMC (Asset Management Company) manages the fund; trustees oversee the AMC.

Key terms:
- NAV (Net Asset Value): Price per unit of the fund, calculated daily after market close
- AUM (Assets Under Management): Total money managed by the fund
- Expense Ratio: Annual fee charged by the fund (deducted from NAV daily)
- Exit Load: Fee charged on redemption within a specified period
- ISIN: International Securities Identification Number — unique fund identifier
- Folio Number: Your unique account number with each AMC

Direct vs Regular Plans:
- Direct Plan: No distributor commission; lower expense ratio (0.1%–0.5% cheaper)
- Regular Plan: Includes distributor commission; higher expense ratio
- Always prefer Direct Plan for long-term wealth creation

Growth vs IDCW (Income Distribution cum Capital Withdrawal):
- Growth: NAV grows; no payouts; suitable for long-term goals
- IDCW: Periodic payouts (dividends); NAV falls by payout amount; IDCW taxable as income
- Prefer Growth option for compounding; IDCW only for regular income needs

SEBI MUTUAL FUND CATEGORIES (AMFI Classification)
================================================================================

EQUITY FUNDS (60%+ in equities; LTCG/STCG apply after 12 months):

Large Cap Fund:
- Invests ≥80% in top 100 companies by market cap
- Lower risk, stable returns; suitable for conservative-moderate investors
- Typical returns: 10-13% CAGR over 5+ years
- Benchmark: Nifty 50, Nifty 100

Mid Cap Fund:
- Invests ≥65% in 101st–250th companies by market cap
- Higher volatility than large cap; higher return potential
- Typical returns: 13-16% CAGR over 5+ years
- Suitable for: moderate-aggressive investors with 7+ year horizon
- Benchmark: Nifty Midcap 150

Small Cap Fund:
- Invests ≥65% in companies below 250th rank
- Highest volatility; significant drawdowns during market downturns
- Typical returns: 15-20% CAGR over 5+ years (but with higher risk)
- Only for aggressive investors with 10+ year horizon

Flexi Cap Fund:
- No restriction on market cap allocation; fund manager decides
- Diversified across large, mid, small cap based on opportunities
- Good all-weather equity fund for most investors

ELSS (Equity Linked Savings Scheme):
- 80% in equities; 3-year lock-in per SIP installment
- Section 80C benefit (old regime): up to ₹1.5 lakh deduction
- LTCG taxable at 12.5% above ₹1.25 lakh annual exemption
- Best: Mirae Asset ELSS, Axis ELSS, Quant ELSS (examples)

Sectoral/Thematic Funds:
- Concentrated in specific sectors (IT, pharma, banking, infrastructure)
- High risk; for tactical allocation only; not for core portfolio
- Requires active monitoring and timely exit

HYBRID FUNDS (Mix of equity + debt):

Balanced Advantage Fund (BAF) / Dynamic Asset Allocation:
- Dynamically shifts between equity and debt based on market valuations
- Lower volatility than pure equity; captures most of upside, limits downside
- LTCG/STCG applies (treated as equity if equity > 65%)
- Suitable for: moderate investors, first-time equity investors

Aggressive Hybrid Fund:
- 65-80% equity, 20-35% debt
- Rebalances automatically; tax-efficient
- Good for moderate-aggressive investors in single fund

Arbitrage Fund:
- 65%+ in equity arbitrage positions; low risk like liquid funds
- Taxed as equity (STCG at 20% if < 12 months; LTCG 12.5% if > 12 months)
- Better than liquid fund for high-tax bracket investors (3+ month horizon)

DEBT FUNDS (Post April 2023 — taxed as income at slab rate):

Liquid Fund:
- Invests in instruments with ≤91-day maturity
- Lowest risk; returns ~7-7.5% (close to repo rate)
- Use for: emergency fund, parking money for 1 week to 3 months
- Exit within 7 days: exit load applies (graded exit load)

Short Duration Fund:
- Macaulay duration 1-3 years
- Returns ~7.5-8%; some interest rate risk
- Use for: 1-3 year goals

Corporate Bond Fund:
- ≥80% in AAA-rated corporate bonds
- Higher returns than gilt; some credit risk
- Returns ~7.5-8.5%

Gilt Fund:
- 80%+ in government securities; zero credit risk
- Interest rate sensitive (NAV falls when rates rise)
- Use only if you understand interest rate cycles

Money Market Fund:
- 1-year maturity instruments (T-bills, CDs, CPs)
- Slightly higher returns than liquid; suitable for 3-6 month horizon

GOLD FUNDS:
Gold ETF: Tracks gold price; stored in demat; 1 unit = 1 gram of gold
Gold Fund of Fund: Invests in Gold ETF; no demat needed; SIP possible
Both taxed as income at slab rate (post Budget 2024 change)
Sovereign Gold Bond (SGB): Best for long-term gold exposure (maturity gains tax-free)

SIP (SYSTEMATIC INVESTMENT PLAN)
================================================================================

What is SIP:
- Fixed amount invested at regular intervals (monthly, weekly, quarterly)
- Rupee cost averaging: buy more units when NAV is low, fewer when NAV is high
- Compounding: returns earned on returns over time
- Discipline: removes need to time the market

SIP vs Lumpsum:
- SIP: Better for regular income investors; reduces timing risk; builds discipline
- Lumpsum: Suitable when markets are undervalued or for windfall investments
- Combination: Lumpsum in corrections + ongoing SIP

Step-Up SIP:
- Increase SIP amount by 10-15% every year (aligned with salary increments)
- Dramatically improves corpus: ₹10,000/month SIP for 20 years at 12% = ₹91L
- Same with 10% annual step-up = ₹1.82 Cr (2x improvement)

SIP calculation formula:
FV = P × [(1+r)^n - 1] / r × (1+r)
Where P = monthly SIP, r = monthly return rate, n = number of months

Recommended SIP allocation:
Conservative investor: 30% large cap equity fund + 70% debt/hybrid
Moderate investor: 60% equity (mix of large/flexi cap) + 40% debt
Aggressive investor: 80% equity (large + mid + small cap) + 20% debt/gold

================================================================================
PORTFOLIO CONSTRUCTION PRINCIPLES
================================================================================

Core-Satellite Approach:
- Core (70-80%): Low-cost index funds or large cap/flexi cap active funds
- Satellite (20-30%): Mid cap, small cap, sectoral for alpha generation

Asset Allocation by age:
- Rule of thumb: Equity % = 100 - Age (e.g., 30 years old → 70% equity)
- More nuanced: Risk tolerance, income stability, goal horizon also matter

Diversification principles:
- Equity diversification: Large (40%) + Mid (30%) + Small (20%) + International (10%)
- Avoid holding too many funds in same category (over-diversification)
- 5-7 funds sufficient for most investors (1-2 in each category)

Rebalancing:
- Restore target allocation at least annually or when any asset class drifts >5%
- Use new investments to rebalance (avoid triggering capital gains)
- December/January: good time for annual rebalancing + LTCG harvesting

================================================================================
IMPORTANT MUTUAL FUND METRICS
================================================================================

XIRR (Extended Internal Rate of Return):
- Most accurate measure of your actual return (accounts for timing of cashflows)
- Better than simple or absolute returns for SIP investors
- Healthy XIRR: >12% for equity over 5+ years; >7% for debt

Expense Ratio:
- Annual cost deducted from NAV daily
- Direct plan: 0.1-0.5% for large cap equity; 0.05-0.1% for index funds
- Regular plan: 0.5-2% (difference goes to distributor as commission)
- Impact: 1% extra expense over 20 years reduces corpus by ~18%

Sharpe Ratio: Risk-adjusted return; higher is better; >1 is good
Sortino Ratio: Downside risk-adjusted return; better than Sharpe for evaluating downside
Alpha: Excess return over benchmark; positive alpha means fund beat its benchmark
Beta: Market sensitivity; beta of 1.2 means fund moves 1.2x the index

Rolling Returns vs Point-to-Point Returns:
- Always check rolling returns (1Y, 3Y, 5Y) over multiple periods
- Point-to-point (e.g., "5-year return as of today") depends heavily on start date
- Consistent rolling returns indicate reliable fund management

================================================================================
KYC AND INVESTMENT PROCESS
================================================================================

KYC (Know Your Customer) — mandatory for all MF investments:
- PAN card + address proof + photograph
- One-time KYC valid across all AMCs (via CAMS/KFintech KYC)
- KYC must be refreshed every 2 years (SEBI requirement)

Investment modes:
- Online: AMC website, MF Central, MFU (MF Utilities), CAMS, KFintech apps
- Demat: Through broker (Zerodha, Groww, etc.); ISIN-based holdings
- NACH/e-NACH: Auto-debit mandate for SIP

Nomination:
- Mandatory for all MF folios from January 2023 (or opt-out affirmatively)
- Add nominee to avoid delay in estate settlement

Redemption:
- T+2 for equity funds (credit to bank account in 2 business days)
- T+1 for debt/liquid funds
- Exit load: typically 1% if redeemed within 1 year for equity funds

================================================================================
COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID
================================================================================

1. Chasing past returns: Last year's top fund is often next year's laggard
2. Too many funds: More than 7-8 funds creates redundancy without diversification
3. Stopping SIP during market falls: This is when SIP works best (buying more units cheap)
4. Ignoring expense ratio: 2% regular plan vs 0.5% direct plan is significant over 20 years
5. Mixing insurance and investment: Avoid ULIPs and endowment plans; buy term insurance separately
6. Redeeming prematurely: Equity funds need 5-7 years to deliver meaningful returns
7. Not reviewing annually: Rebalance and review fund performance once a year
